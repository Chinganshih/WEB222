Let's learn Blue Jay - Behavior&Voice!


Voice:

There are also a variety of other calls blue jays use occasionally, such as a mellow whistle, kloo-loo-loo, and a soft, melodious song sung along with a warbling sound during courtship.
To warn other birds and mammals of an approaching predator, it often issues loud cries, and these cries are often without apparent reason.
The sound of the Blue Jay is described as being like a "steel-cold scream," sometimes referred to as "thief," "jay," or "peer." The Blue Jay’s call is a familiar woodland sound in eastern North America.

Behavior:

A variety of blue jay calls carry over long distances. Jays usually make their calls while perched within trees.
Usually flies across open areas silently, especially during migration. Food is stored in the throat pouch, then removed to be eaten elsewhere.
When eating, it holds a seed or nut in its feet and pecks it open.

A blue jay communicates with another through both voice and body language. Birds hold down their crest when incubating, feeding nestlings, and associating with mates, families, or flock mates.
The streamlined crest represents a bird's reduced aggression level.
Blue Jays tend to squawk more when their crests are held up; when they squawk the crest is nearly always raised.
They have an extensive range of vocalizations.

Approximately three to four acorns are stored in the bird's throat, one in their mouth, and one in the tip of its bill.
In this way they can carry off 5 acorns at a time to store for later feeding.

The female broods the nestlings for the first 8-12 days after they hatch, and the male provides food for both he and his mate.
Following this point, females and males share food gathering, but male provide more food.
A few days before the brood flies, some nestlings begin wandering 15 feet from the nest.
